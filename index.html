making the math real

In plain numbers.

The instruments above use standard language (revenue share, convertible note, SAFE). That can feel abstract. Here's what those structures actually mean for you in real dollars, using the conservative projections from Business Plan V2.1.

starter · $1,000 example

You put in $1,000. You get a 0.5% share of the template product line for 2 years.

If templates hit the conservative projection ($6K to $14K in Year 1, $18K to $30K in Year 2), your return is roughly $150 to $220 over 2 years.

Honest framing: this tier is goodwill with a small upside, not a return-optimized investment. The heart of it is supporting the build. The cap is $1,500 max, which the business would have to clear ambitious projections to reach.

core · $5,000 example (template line)

You put in $5,000 into the template production line. You get a 5% share of that line for 3 years.

At moderate projections (templates growing to ~$30K/year by Year 3), your return is roughly $2,700 to $6,500 over 3 years.

At ambitious projections, the line hits the 2x cap, returning up to $10,000.

This is a real return, sized to a specific product line. You see where your money went. You can track what it did.

deep · $25,000 example

You put in $25,000 split across three lines ($10K production, $10K consultant, $5K runway). You pick one structure:

  • Blended revenue share: roughly $12,000 to $22,000 return over 4 years at moderate projections. Cap at $50,000 if ambitious projections hit.
  • Convertible note: the $25K stays a low-interest loan (1-2%) unless a priced round happens. If it does, your $25K converts to equity at a 20% discount to the round price.
  • SAFE: your $25K converts to equity at a capped valuation if and when a priced round happens. Quiet until then.

Convertible note and SAFE are startup instruments, standard in seed-stage deals. They matter if the practice ever does a proper raise (Year 2 or later in the plan). If it never does, the note stays a loan.
